Unstructured. Written during the philosophy extraction session, 2026-06-13.

## What are humans capable of? — 2026-06-13

Humans are capable of absolutely anything. The greatest greatness and the most evil evil.
What we're failing to reach isn't just heights we've reached before — heights never
reached before. The ceiling is infinite. Nietzsche's Übermensch.

We're bogged down. Held back by current society and the institutions we've succumbed to.
Unable to do the things we truly need to do.

We can't love freely. Our direction is given to us so strongly. To survive now means to
work. To work for the people in charge. We don't choose our direction — it chooses us.
We are bred to work. Not to follow our passions or our loves.

Love means: love of people, love of craft, love of life itself. Genuine love. The kind
that shows up in full presence — being with friends, fully in the moment, not caring
about anything else. That's when the ceiling lifts.

Society makes that unsustainable. You can't live there. You have to get a job. You have
to work. The thing that makes humans most alive is structurally impossible to sustain.

Society is optimized for throughput. Not quality of output — pure throughput. Not for
living, not for love, not for purpose. The reward function for the model is wrong.

---

## The right reward function — 2026-06-13

The US Declaration had it: life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness.

That was the founding ideal. It got corrupted into pure productivity anyway. The failure
mechanism: capitalism and greed. Not just systemic — the inherent evil in all people.
The same duality that makes humans capable of infinite greatness makes them capable of
corruption. Both are always present. The Bible teaches it. Clarke believes it.

What tips the balance? Morals. Good teaching. Values instilled early. Family, teachers,
media — the Grateful Dead, Dead Poets Society, comic books, Pink Floyd, Ferris Bueller,
Nietzsche, Marcus Aurelius. None alone. All together. A mosaic.

The actual value they collectively point at: **DOING THE RIGHT THING**. Whether you want
to or not, whether it's easy or not. Just always being a good person.

Why don't more people do it? It's not profitable — not monetarily, not socially, at
least not in the short term. People can't be bothered. It's not easy.

Spider-Man: with great power comes great responsibility. No one wants responsibility.

## Why Clarke still tries — 2026-06-13

He's not the exception. There are millions of good people. The exceptions aren't rare —
there are just too few structures that let them act.

Clarke's reason for building Mojo at the philosophical level: AI will change everything.
A lot of it is absolutely terrifying. He wants to see if he can steer us in a direction
that helps us be better. Not just freer. Better.

---

## The dark future — 2026-06-13

Many possibilities. The one that scares most: nothing matters anymore. Everything
becomes a sludge of AI-generated slop. Dead internet theory creeps into the real world.
Everything is artificial. There is no truth. 1984 from governments. Depression, anxiety,
suicide rates keep rising. Loss of true humanity.

True humanity: connection. Face-to-face human connection where nothing else matters,
all else melts away. Not artificial. Not online. Real presence.

The dark future isn't AI killing connection directly — it's AI turbocharging the same
disease that was already killing it. The productivity machine was already eating
connection before AI existed. AI just makes it faster.

## The bell curve — 2026-06-13

Unprompted thought: humanity probably follows a bell curve over time. Population had
to increase to increase output and get to where we are now. But at some point it
tapers back off — leaving just the lucky in paradise.

Why? You don't require humans in a world of AI.

Clarke's word for it: terrifying. He named it himself. Didn't read it anywhere.

The question it raises: who decides who the lucky ones are? Right now — the big
companies and people in power.

---

## Everyone in the room — 2026-06-13

Mojo isn't just a bid to change who decides. It's a bid to put everyone in the room.

Dario Amodei has said more people need to be involved in the AI conversation. If
everyone has their own personal AI that can work together — everyone can join the
field. Our AIs fight for what we believe in. Led by us. Joined into the collective.

Democratic at the AI layer, not just the political layer.

But that only works if the underlying models aren't controlled by the same four
companies. Open source is the only answer. And it's coming fast. Open source is
catching up. And if you can get all the individual people and their AIs into one big
collective, tasked with working on and maintaining the models — maybe that helps.

## The wall — 2026-06-13

Near the end of the session, Clarke hit the same wall he'd been describing the whole
time. The thing that's not easy, not profitable, that nobody wants to carry.

Direct quote: "I'm starting to wonder if there is even any point in me developing

Worth holding. Not as a crisis but as the honest version of what he said earlier:
doing the right thing is hard, no one wants responsibility, people can't be bothered.
He ran into that in real time.

Also said: "The philosophy lives in millions of memories within me." The feeling is
bigger than what can be articulated. The extraction session is trying to close that gap.

Clarke's read: needs to step back for a week. Let things settle. Decide what he's
actually trying to make. Come back fresh.

Distinction Clarke needs to hold: doubt about the scope (is collective intelligence
viable and buildable?) is a different question from doubt about the philosophy (do
I actually believe this?). Don't let one bleed into the other.
