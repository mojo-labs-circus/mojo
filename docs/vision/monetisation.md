# How Mojo Makes Money

Exploratory thinking. Not a business plan — just capturing what's consistent with the
project's values and what the natural growth path looks like.

---

## The Constraint

Mojo cannot monetise by centralising data or selling access to user data. That kills
the project at the root — the entire value proposition is sovereignty, and you can't
sell sovereignty while extracting the thing that makes it real. Any revenue model has
to work with the open, sovereign architecture, not around it.

The clean version of this: the open source core stays open. Money comes from the
services, hardware, and expertise around it. This is a proven model — Red Hat did it
with Linux, Canonical with Ubuntu. The software being free is not the problem, it's
the feature that builds the audience.

---

## The Near-Term Revenue Streams

### 1. Hardware — sell the server

The most direct path. Pre-built Ringmaster boxes: right hardware, right spec, MojOS
installed, ready to plug in. Someone who believes in the sovereignty argument but
doesn't want to source and configure their own hardware pays for the convenience.

This is honest money — you're selling a physical thing that makes the sovereign system
accessible without requiring technical depth. The buyer gets a working system. You get
margin on the hardware and the setup work.

As the homelab and local AI markets grow, demand for "just works" hardware grows with
them. You're not competing with NVIDIA or Apple — you're competing with the friction
of doing it yourself.

### 2. Setup and support for collectives

A household collective takes real work to configure well: Tailscale network, machine
registration, user accounts, memory config, model selection for the hardware profile.
A research group or small company collective takes more. Charge for doing it properly.

Ongoing support is a natural extension: updates, troubleshooting, model upgrades,
adding new machines to the collective. Annual support contracts are clean recurring
revenue and don't compromise the architecture.

### 3. The AI house — a sibling market

The Roman bath analogy plays out commercially here. SPAN/XFRA is putting compute in
homes but it's their server, not yours. The gap in the market is: a home with a
Ringmaster built in — proper rack, power, cooling, ethernet — sold as a feature the
way fibre connectivity or a home cinema is sold as a feature.

This is a partnership play rather than a direct product: work with homebuilders or
high-end smart-home integrators to include a Mojo-ready server room as a standard
option. You provide the spec, the software, the setup. They provide the homes.
The buyer gets a house with a personal AI system already running.

This is further out, but the market is forming. SPAN proved homebuilders will partner
on this. The difference is selling the sovereign version.

---

## The Longer-Term Picture

As compute costs drop and local AI tooling matures, the technically-capable-only
constraint loosens and the addressable market grows. The revenue mix shifts:

- Hardware margin stays healthy as demand broadens
- Support becomes more systematised, less bespoke
- A managed setup tier becomes viable — Mojo running in your home, Clarke's team
  managing updates and support remotely, but your data never leaving your hardware
- Potentially: a certification programme for other people to set up and support
  Mojo collectives, with a franchise-style model

The goal is not a massive company. A profitable, sovereign, bootstrapped business
that serves a loyal audience well — one person or a small team, high margin, no VC
pressure, no compromise on the architecture. That's a real outcome and it's more
achievable than the VC route without requiring you to become a different kind of
founder.

---

## What Not To Do

- No SaaS play that stores user data centrally
- No freemium model where the premium tier requires sending data to Mojo's servers
- No VC funding that comes with pressure to grow into a market Mojo isn't for
- No pivoting the open source core into a paid product — the community is the moat

## Summary

| Stream | When | Model |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-built hardware | Mk1 onwards | Direct sales, hardware margin |
| Setup + support | Mk1 onwards | Project fees + annual contracts |
| AI house partnerships | Post-Mk1, as market forms | Partnership / referral |
| Managed remote support | When audience broadens | Recurring subscription |
