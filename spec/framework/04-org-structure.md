# Session 04 — Collective Structure

> Defines how intelligences organise themselves into a collective: the shape of
> the group, how authority flows, how ownership is distributed.

---

## Purpose

Design the structure of a collective built on this framework. How individual
intelligences cluster into groups, how those groups relate to each other, how
authority and ownership are distributed, and how the structure holds together
as the collective grows or changes.

This is not an org chart imposed from outside. It is the natural structure that
emerges when intelligences organise toward a shared goal — and which this framework
tries to model correctly.

---

## Context

From first principles we understand why intelligences form collectives and what
patterns emerge universally. From the agent model we know what an individual
intelligence looks like. This session defines how those intelligences combine into
a coherent, functional collective.

The structure is a graph. Nodes are intelligences. Edges are relationships —
authority, trust, communication, dependency, ownership. The shape of that graph
determines what the collective can do, how it adapts, and how it fails.

---

## Questions to Resolve

**The shape of the graph**

- What node types exist in a collective? What is the minimum taxonomy?
- What edge types exist? What are the valid relationships between intelligences?
- How many tiers naturally emerge — and does that depend on collective size,
  goal complexity, or something else?
- What is the natural size limit for a sub-group before it needs to split?
  (Dunbar's layers are relevant evidence here.)
- How does the graph change as the collective scales up or down?

**Sub-groups**

- Why do sub-groups form — is it always specialisation, or are there other forces?
- How is a sub-group defined? By domain, by function, by output type, by proximity?
- How do sub-groups remain aware of each other without becoming entangled?
- Is sub-group structure fixed at collective formation or does it evolve?

**Ownership and boundaries**

- What does it mean for an intelligence (or sub-group) to "own" something within
  the collective?
- What happens at ownership boundaries — how do intelligences from different
  sub-groups interact without undermining each other?
- Who resolves conflicts at a boundary?

**Authority and trust**

- How is authority distributed in a collective? Is it always hierarchical, or
  can other structures work?
- How does trust between intelligences develop — and what happens when it breaks?
- Can authority be delegated — and if so, how far down the graph?

**Participation model**

- Are participants persistent (long-running members of the collective) or ephemeral
  (joining for a specific contribution then dissolving)?
- Can both coexist in the same collective? When is each appropriate?
- How does a new participant learn the collective's shared context quickly enough
  to contribute?

**Human-AI structure**

- Where do human intelligences sit in the graph relative to AI intelligences?
  Always at the top? Peer nodes? Somewhere else?
- Is human membership structurally different from AI membership — or is it
  just a difference in capability profile?
- What does it mean for a human to be "in" a collective that is largely AI-operated?
  Are they always present, or do they interact episodically?
- How does the structure change when the ratio of human to AI participants shifts
  dramatically in either direction?

**Escalation**

- When an intelligence hits a decision outside its authority, what is the path?
- Up the graph? Sideways? Directly to the top?
- What is the protocol when the collective stalls — no one can unblock without
  a decision from above?

---

## Generalisation Test

Verify the structure is genuinely general. It must model all of these:

- A software dev team
- A sports team
- A creative ensemble (band, film crew)
- A research group

If the structure requires domain-specific exceptions, the abstraction is wrong.

---

## Output Required

1. Node type taxonomy — every valid participant role, generically defined
2. Edge type taxonomy — every valid relationship type between nodes
3. Tier model — how many tiers, what each tier does, scaling rules
4. Sub-group model — how sub-groups form, what defines them, how they stay coherent
5. Ownership model — what ownership means, boundary protocol, conflict resolution
6. Participation model — persistent vs ephemeral, onboarding into shared context
7. Escalation protocol — full path from blocked participant to resolution
