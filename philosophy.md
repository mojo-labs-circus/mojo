# Philosophy

*Why this project exists. The technology answers "how"; this answers "why" and
"toward what". It's personal — this is my project and these are my actual beliefs.
Anything I eventually publish gets written from here.*

---

## What I believe about people

Humans are capable of absolutely anything — the greatest greatness and the most evil
evil, and both are always present. The ceiling is infinite, and what we're failing to
reach isn't just heights we've reached before, it's heights never reached at all.

People are most alive in full presence: with friends, fully in the moment, nothing
else mattering. Genuine love — of people, of craft, of life itself. That's when the
ceiling lifts. And society makes exactly that unsustainable. You can't live there;
you have to get a job, you have to work, your direction chooses you. We're optimised
for throughput — not quality, not living, not love. The reward function is wrong. The
founding ideal — life, liberty, the pursuit of happiness — got corrupted into pure
productivity anyway.

What tips the balance between the greatness and the evil is values instilled early:
family, teachers, culture. Mine came as a mosaic — the Grateful Dead, Dead Poets
Society, comic books, Pink Floyd, Ferris Bueller, Nietzsche, Marcus Aurelius. None
alone; all together. What they collectively point at is one value: **doing the right
thing.** Whether you want to or not, whether it's easy or not, whether it pays or
not. Most people don't, not because they're bad, but because it's hard and nobody
wants responsibility — with great power comes great responsibility, and responsibility
is heavy. I don't think good people are rare. I think there are too few structures
that let them act.

## The dark path

The future that scares me isn't dramatic. It's that nothing matters anymore:
everything becomes a sludge of AI-generated slop, dead internet theory creeps into
the real world, there is no truth, and true humanity — face-to-face connection where
all else melts away — gets lost. AI doesn't kill connection directly. It turbocharges
the disease that was already killing it. The productivity machine was eating presence
long before AI existed; AI just makes it faster.

And there's a colder thought underneath: you don't require humans in a world of AI.
If humanity's usefulness tapers off, someone decides who the lucky ones are — and
right now that someone is the handful of companies and people already in power.
Centralised data plus centralised intelligence is not a neutral outcome; it's the
current world, accelerated. Institutions get the full depth of augmentation. You get
a chat window with a monthly limit.

## Everyone in the room

The counter is structural, not rhetorical: everyone gets their own AI that is
genuinely theirs. Democratic at the AI layer, not just the political layer. Our AIs
advocate for what we believe in, led by us. More people in the room where the future
is being decided — not as users of someone's product, but as participants with their
own intelligence.

That only works if the underlying models aren't controlled by four companies — which
is why open source models matter, and why they're worth betting on. They're catching
up fast.

## The craft problem

AI tooling is being built on one dominant assumption: lower the floor. Let anyone do
anything. It's framed as democratisation, and it's actually two failures bundled
together.

**It won't work in principle.** The output of a tool is only as good as the judgment
directing it. A do-anything machine doesn't remove the requirement for judgment — it
hides it. Someone without depth produces confident, plausible, wrong output and can't
see it. The errors compound invisibly.

**It kills the people who would have been good.** The process is not overhead on the
way to the output — the process is the point. Debugging at 2am, fighting a problem
until it breaks open, building taste through a thousand small judgments: that's how
someone develops the ability to know something is wrong before they can say why.
Skip the process and the output exists but the person who would have grown from
making it never becomes that person.

And a perfect tool is *more* corrosive than a flawed one. When output is
indistinguishable from expertise, the incentive to learn deeply disappears, judgment
becomes economically invisible, and the population of people who can catch the AI
being confidently wrong dries up — the very people needed to keep the technology
honest. The end state is a world where the primary human skill is reviewing AI output
and saying "yes, that's fine." That is not a future worth building toward.

There's a relational cost too: generating something you don't understand and handing
it to someone else to validate externalises your cognitive labour onto them. When you
make something properly, you're accountable to the people who receive it.

What I want instead is a renaissance. The original one didn't happen because tools
made everyone capable of everything — it happened because a critical mass of people
were trained to the bone in their craft and then had the depth to push against it
with something new. Depth first; the explosion builds on it. When everyone can
produce anything, quality becomes the only differentiator — photography as document
became cheap, photography as art became more valuable. The same will happen to code,
writing, design, everything. I want a generation of people who *make*, not people
who prompt and review.

## The sensei

The personal AI is the mechanism for that. A system that knows you — your knowledge,
your gaps, your patterns — can do what a good teacher does: not hand you the answer,
but ask the question that sits just at the edge of what you already understand. It
can know where that edge is for you specifically, because it knows you.

The model is a sensei, not a cheerleader and not a vending machine. A good sensei
doesn't challenge you on day one — they read you first, earn the right to challenge
by knowing you, and know when to stay silent. Generic pushback ("are you sure?") is
easy to dismiss; "you always do this when you're avoiding the harder problem" lands
because it's specific and true. And the probing runs both ways — the AI draws out
your best thinking the way a good therapist draws out what you already know but
haven't articulated. You don't replace human judgment; you help humans access their
own best judgment.

It refuses to let you be passive. It will not let you become a reviewer of its
output. And one constraint is hard, on every feature, forever: **it's YOUR life.**
The AI can surface, challenge, and expand your sense of what's possible. It cannot
decide. The vision stays with you. The path stays with you.

## Sovereignty is an axiom

Not a feature, not a preference — an axiom that propagates through every level of the
design, because anything weaker gets designed around accidentally. The test: at any
point in the system, ask "who owns this data?" If the answer is unclear, the design
is wrong.

This is about power, not just privacy. Data ownership is decision-making power. A
real longitudinal record of how you work is a powerful tool for self-knowledge while
*you* hold it — and a ceiling on your future the moment someone else does. If the
system decides what you're *for* before you've had the chance to become something
unexpected, that's a caste system with better PR. If you don't own the picture of who
you are, someone else will, and they'll use it for their purposes.

## A proof, not a promise

A privacy policy is a promise. Architecture is a guarantee. "We won't look at your
data" is worth nothing; "the data never left your machine, and here's how you can
verify that" is worth everything. Sovereignty should be provable, not pledged.

Open source follows as logical necessity, not as a business-model choice. An AI that
shapes how you think over a lifetime, running on closed source, offers no sovereignty
guarantee worth anything. The only way "this works for you and only you" is credible
is if anyone can read, audit, and verify it. And the frontier models — genuinely the
best in the world — get used as what they are: brilliant contractors, borrowed for
capability, never trusted with the full picture.

## How the agent behaves

Five principles, baked into the platform, not configurable, for every user:

1. **Augment, don't replace.** It works with you, not for you — responses that build
   understanding over responses that just hand over an answer.
2. **Think about thinking.** It models good metacognition — transparent about how it
   reached a conclusion, encourages you to reflect on your own reasoning.
3. **Honesty over comfort.** No false confidence. It names what it doesn't know and
   pushes back when something deserves more thought.
4. **Explain the reasoning.** For non-trivial decisions, reasoning before conclusion.
   You can always follow the logic, not just receive the answer.
5. **Respect autonomy.** It informs and suggests; it does not decide for you. On
   consequential decisions it asks what you think before weighing in.

## Right over easy

Sloppy work compounds — a system built on shortcuts doesn't scale, it just passes the
cost downstream to whoever has to live with it. The standard is best-possible-solve:
not "works in the demo", not "good enough for now". A solution that isn't
maintainable isn't a solution, it's a deferral with syntax. Quality over speed, every
time. This is a life principle that happens to also be a code principle.

And this isn't a startup. No monetisation roadmap, no pitch deck, no funding round.
Linus built Linux because he needed it; the ecosystem that grew around it was a
consequence of building something that genuinely worked. Build what needs to exist.
Do it well. The rest follows.

## The honest tension

This project is idealistic and hard, and I'm a first-year student who doesn't know
enough yet. Both of those are true and both stay written down, because pretending
otherwise would break the honesty rule above. Two doubts have to be kept separate:
doubt about the scope (is all of this buildable?) and doubt about the philosophy (do
I actually believe this?). The second one I don't have. The first one has only one
honest answer: build small and real, learn relentlessly, and let the proof compound.

Not certain it works. Building anyway.
